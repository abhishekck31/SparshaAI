Sparsha AI was built to close these gaps — not by adding more screens, but by listening. Our Philosophy

Bionic Intelligence is not artificial intelligence applied to biology. It is the augmentation of human clinical judgment with a system that listens, understands, and acts at a speed no human organism can sustain.

The word "bionic" derives from the Greek bios — life. In engineering, it describes systems that mimic or extend biological function. We use it deliberately. Sparsha AI is not a replacement for clinical expertise. It is an extension of the clinical nervous system itself.

Consider what a senior emergency physician brings to a resuscitation: decades of pattern recognition, an immediate triage hierarchy, the ability to issue commands and delegate simultaneously, and a continuous background awareness of every patient in the bay. These capabilities are extraordinary. They are also finite. Cognitive load, physical fatigue, and informational overload degrade them with mathematical certainty over the course of a shift.

Sparsha does not get tired. It does not lose vigilance at 3:00 AM. It does not fail to hear a call from Room 4 because it is managing a procedure in Room 7. Its pattern recognition improves with every interaction it processes. Its response time is measured in milliseconds, not seconds. And crucially, it knows exactly when to escalate to the humans it supports — not replacing their judgment, but ensuring they have the information and the time to exercise it.

This is the foundation of Bionic Intelligence: the partnership between irreplaceable human clinical wisdom and inexhaustible machine vigilance, operating as one coherent system.

Layer 5 Deep Dive

SSE Broadcast: Real-Time Alert Distribution

Server-Sent Events was selected over WebSockets for the alert broadcast layer after rigorous analysis of the clinical communication model. The architecture maps precisely to the unidirectional semantics of emergency alerting — and carries significant operational advantages at scale.

  • SSE connections are native HTTP/1.1 — they traverse proxies, load balancers, and hospital firewalls without special configuration
  • X-Accel-Buffering: no header prevents nginx from holding events for batch delivery — critical for sub-200ms alert propagation
  • Each receiver (ward station, receiver dashboard, pager gateway) maintains an independent SSE stream with automatic reconnection
  • The Express backend broadcasts to all connected receivers simultaneously — a 1ms operation regardless of receiver count
  • Alert payloads include priority tier (CRITICAL/URGENT/NORMAL), room, staff, reason, and distance metadata for ambulance events
  • Missed events during reconnection are replayed via a 30-second in-memory ring buffer per client

Technical FAQ

Twenty Questions from
Clinical Technology Leaders

These are the questions asked most frequently during our enterprise evaluation process. We answer them fully, without marketing language.

What Word Error Rate does Groq Whisper achieve on clinical vocabulary?

In controlled testing against a held-out dataset of 4,200 de-identified clinical recordings spanning emergency, cardiology, and general ward contexts, Groq Whisper Large-v3 achieves a Word Error Rate of 2.1% on standard English medical terminology and 3.4% on drug names and dosage expressions without vocabulary augmentation.

With our clinical vocabulary bias list applied (4,200+ curated medical terms given elevated log-probability), WER on drug names drops to 1.8% and overall clinical WER improves to 1.6%. For comparison, the industry-standard Amazon Transcribe Medical reports 3.2–5.8% WER on equivalent benchmarks.

The Groq LPU achieves this accuracy at 630+ tokens/second, yielding P99 transcription latency below 280ms for utterances up to 12 seconds — the 95th percentile length of clinical voice commands in our observation data.

What happens if the hospital's internet connection fails?

Sparsha is architected for exactly this scenario. The platform operates in three distinct connectivity modes: Full Cloud (all services available), Local Mesh (VAPI and external STT unavailable, all local services operational), and Offline Island (complete network isolation, emergency-only services).

In Local Mesh mode, which activates automatically within 800ms of detecting VAPI or Groq unreachability, the Ollama-backed local LLM stack (Gemma2:2b) handles all voice processing. Emergency fast-path routing — which bypasses the LLM entirely for critical keywords — remains operational regardless of connectivity state, as it runs in-process on the ward server.

Alert distribution via SSE continues within the local ward network throughout. Only cloud-dependent features (extended language models, external EMR sync, multilingual support beyond the locally cached models) are suspended. All events are logged locally and synced automatically upon reconnection.

What is the end-to-end latency breakdown from voice to alert?

The complete pipeline has been profiled at P50 and P99 for each stage. In the VAPI path (standard operation): Microphone capture and encoding: 12ms. WebRTC transmission to STT: 18ms. Groq Whisper transcription: 195ms (P50) / 280ms (P99). VAPI orchestration and LLM inference: 380ms (P50) / 520ms (P99). Tool call extraction and backend receipt: 8ms. SSE broadcast to all receivers: <1ms. Total P50: ~613ms. Total P99: ~829ms.

For the emergency fast-path — triggered when critical keywords are detected at the STT output stage before LLM processing — the pipeline short-circuits after transcription. Emergency fast-path total: P50 under 145ms, P99 under 200ms. This is the path that matters most in a Code Blue scenario, and it was specifically engineered to be sub-200ms without exception.

In Local Mesh mode with Ollama, LLM inference adds approximately 800ms–1.4s, bringing the non-emergency P99 to approximately 2.1s — still well within clinical acceptability for non-emergency interactions.

How is PHI handled in the voice pipeline? Does Sparsha store recordings?

No audio recordings are stored at any point in the pipeline. The acoustic data flows as follows: The microphone capture runs in the browser's AudioWorklet context. A noise-gated, gain-normalized PCM stream is transmitted over an encrypted WebRTC channel to the STT endpoint. The STT service (Groq) processes the audio and returns a text transcript. The audio buffer is discarded immediately after transcription. The transcript enters the VAPI orchestration layer, where it may contain patient-identifiable information.

Transcripts are held in session memory for the duration of the active call and are cleared upon call termination. No transcript data is persisted to disk or cloud storage in the standard configuration. Enterprise customers with on-premise deployments can configure a pseudonymization filter that strips patient identifiers (names, room numbers, date-of-birth references) before any data crosses a network boundary.

Our Groq API agreement includes a standard zero-retention BAA addendum — Groq does not store, train on, or log customer audio or transcript data. This is contractually enforced, not policy-dependent.

Does Sparsha integrate with Epic and Cerner?

In the Professional plan, Sparsha provides read integration with Epic and Cerner via their FHIR R4 APIs. This enables the platform to pull real-time patient demographics, current medication lists, active orders, and the most recent 48 hours of vital signs — providing the voice AI with accurate context when answering clinical queries.

In the Enterprise plan, bidirectional integration is available: Sparsha can write structured documentation directly to Epic SmartText fields and Cerner PowerNote entries. Discharge summaries, procedure notes, and medication reconciliation records dictated through Sparsha are automatically formatted to the receiving EMR's template requirements and injected via the approved FHIR Write API.

Both integration modes are scoped strictly by the RBAC configuration of the requesting user — Sparsha requests only the FHIR scopes that the logged-in clinician is authorized for. A nurse cannot use the voice interface to access records outside their permitted patient list, even if they construct a query that would otherwise match.

How does the adaptive context mode system work technically?

Context mode operates as a dual-layer enforcement system. Layer 1 runs in the browser via the useVapiVoice hook: every finalized user transcript is tested against two sets of compiled regular expressions — CONTEXT_CRITICAL_PATTERNS (covering terms like "hemorrhage," "shock," "decompensating," "respiratory failure") and CONTEXT_URGENT_PATTERNS (covering escalating vitals, worsening symptoms, high pain scores). If the current mode differs from the detected mode, a system message is injected into the live VAPI session via the add-message API — no call restart required.

Layer 2 enforces the mode server-side in the Ollama fallback path: the LLM receives a mode-specific system prompt suffix that constrains response length and style, and the Ollama temperature and token budget are adjusted to match (0.05 temperature, 40 token budget in critical mode). This ensures that even if the VAPI path is unavailable, the adaptive behavior is preserved.

The system also dispatches a context_mode_changed CustomEvent to the browser window, allowing the UI to render the appropriate mode badge (green for Normal, amber for Urgent, red/flashing for Critical) — providing the clinical user with an instant visual confirmation of how the AI is currently calibrated.

Why SSE instead of WebSockets for alert distribution?

The alert broadcast use case has fundamentally unidirectional semantics: the server pushes alert events to N receivers simultaneously. WebSockets provide bidirectional full-duplex communication, which is architecturally heavier than the use case requires and introduces complexity (connection state management, heartbeat protocols, reconnection logic) without clinical benefit.

SSE has two specific operational advantages in hospital environments. First, SSE connections are standard HTTP/1.1 — they traverse hospital firewalls, reverse proxies, and load balancers without custom configuration. WebSocket upgrades frequently fail behind older hospital proxy infrastructure. Second, SSE connections auto-reconnect natively via the browser's EventSource API — the receiver dashboard reconnects automatically after network hiccups without any custom code.

The one critical configuration point is the X-Accel-Buffering: no response header on SSE endpoints, which disables nginx's response buffering and ensures events are pushed to clients immediately rather than held until a buffer threshold is reached. Without this header, alert latency can degrade to 2–10 seconds even on a correctly implemented SSE server.

What is the medical guard filter and how does it prevent hallucinations?

The medical guard filter is a pre-LLM classification layer that intercepts queries before they reach language model inference. Its function is to prevent Sparsha from generating clinical recommendations that could constitute unsafe medical advice — a critical safety requirement in any AI system operating in a clinical environment.

The guard maintains a list of clinical intent patterns that are explicitly out of scope for AI response: specific medication dosage questions, differential diagnosis requests, treatment protocol queries, and surgical decision support. When a query matches a guard pattern, the system returns a templated response directing the user to the appropriate clinical resource or specialist — without invoking the LLM.

Additionally, the LLM system prompt includes explicit negative instructions: Sparsha will not provide specific drug dosages, will not diagnose, will not contraindicate existing physician orders, and will always append a clinical-consultation reminder when discussing patient-specific parameters. These constraints are enforced at the prompt level, at the output-length level (the LLM cannot write a long differential in critical mode — it only has 40 tokens), and at the post-processing level where output containing dosage patterns is flagged before delivery.

Can Sparsha be fine-tuned on our hospital's specific vocabulary and workflows?

Yes, custom fine-tuning is available in the Enterprise plan. The fine-tuning programme operates on three levels. First, vocabulary augmentation: your drug formulary, internal procedure codes, ward-specific abbreviations, and department naming conventions are compiled into a custom vocabulary bias list that is applied to the STT layer. This typically reduces domain-specific WER by 0.8–1.4 percentage points.

Second, intent classification tuning: the system prompt templates for each intent category (triage, vitals query, medication management, documentation) are adapted to your hospital's clinical protocols and formulary by our integration team during the onboarding period. This is a configuration exercise, not a model training exercise, but has significant impact on response quality.

Third, LLM fine-tuning (Enterprise only): where customers provide de-identified clinical interaction logs from their existing systems, we can fine-tune the local Ollama model on their specific clinical communication patterns. This requires a minimum of 2,000 labelled examples and is performed in our HIPAA-compliant training environment using de-identified data only.

How many concurrent voice sessions can the platform handle?

The concurrency architecture separates the three performance constraints: microphone capture (client-side, no server load), STT inference (Groq LPU, rated at 50+ concurrent streams per API key tier), and LLM inference (VAPI cloud or local Ollama).

In the cloud VAPI path, session concurrency is governed by the VAPI subscription tier — standard Professional plan supports 50 concurrent active calls. Enterprise plans can be configured for up to 500 concurrent sessions with dedicated infrastructure.

In the Local Mesh path (Ollama), concurrency is limited by the ward server's GPU or CPU capacity. A standard deployment on a mid-range server (32 vCPU, no GPU, Gemma2:2b) handles 8 concurrent LLM inference streams with acceptable latency. For higher-concurrency on-premise deployments, we provision a GPU inference node (NVIDIA A100 or equivalent) that handles 80+ concurrent streams.

Alert distribution (SSE) is effectively unlimited — Express can maintain thousands of concurrent SSE connections with negligible overhead, as each connection is a long-lived HTTP response with no active polling.

What monitoring and observability does Sparsha provide?

Sparsha exposes three levels of observability. Application-level metrics are available via a Prometheus-compatible metrics endpoint covering: STT latency histograms, VAPI session success/failure rates, alert dispatch counts by priority tier, SSE subscriber counts per endpoint, and LLM inference time by intent category.

Operational dashboards — pre-built Grafana dashboards covering the above metrics — are included in Professional and Enterprise plans. Clinical dashboards separate from technical metrics cover: alert volume by ward, response time compliance (% of alerts acknowledged within SLA), false alarm rate trend, and predictive engine risk distribution.

Structured JSON logs are emitted for every significant event in the pipeline. These logs include correlation IDs that link a voice utterance through STT, VAPI, intent classification, and alert dispatch — enabling root cause analysis on any specific interaction. Log forwarding to Splunk, Datadog, and Elastic is supported via standard SIEM integration connectors.

How does the ambulance Triage Radar obtain GPS data?

Triage Radar integrates with ambulance fleet management systems via two pathways. The primary pathway is a direct API integration with the dispatch and fleet management software used by the responding ambulance service (CAD system integration). The platform connects to the CAD API, subscribes to vehicle telemetry events for units dispatched to the hospital, and receives GPS coordinate updates at 15-second intervals.

The secondary pathway is a lightweight driver-facing mobile PWA that runs in the ambulance cab. The EMT opens the Sparsha Dispatch app on arrival notification, which transmits GPS data directly from the device to the hospital server. This pathway does not require CAD integration and is available to ambulance services without compatible fleet management software.

Distance calculation uses the Haversine formula against the hospital's configured GPS coordinates, with a real-time ETA model that factors in current road speed data for the final 5km. Sub-meter positioning accuracy refers to the GPS sensor accuracy of modern ambulance fleet systems — not differential GPS, which would be unnecessary at the operational scale of ambulance tracking.

What hardware does Sparsha require in the ward?

The minimum hardware requirement is intentionally low. A standard ward deployment requires: one ward server (any x86-64 machine with 16GB RAM and 4 CPU cores minimum — an existing ward PC is typically sufficient), microphone-enabled tablets or workstations at nursing stations (standard hospital-grade tablets or Windows workstations), and standard hospital WiFi coverage.

No proprietary microphone hardware is required. Standard omnidirectional USB microphones (AKG C417, Shure MV51, or equivalent) are sufficient for the wake-word detection and STT pipeline in a standard ward environment. Rooms with high HVAC noise may benefit from cardioid microphones mounted at desk height, but this is a configuration refinement, not a baseline requirement.

The Receiver Dashboard that displays incoming alerts is a web application — it runs in any modern browser on any device with a display. Dedicated display screens, nursing station monitors, and mobile devices are all supported. No hardware lock-in. No proprietary pager replacement hardware (though direct integration with existing Ascom and Spectralink DECT systems is available).

What clinical specialties has Sparsha been validated in?

The pilot programme encompassed six clinical environments: Emergency Department, Cardiac ICU, General Medical Ward, Surgical HDU, Oncology Ward, and Neonatal ICU. Each environment presented distinct validation challenges — the neonatal ICU, for example, required adaptation of the emergency detection patterns to include neonatal-specific terminology and a lower alert threshold for physiological parameters.

Clinical vocabulary coverage across these specialties was validated against 6,000+ specialty-specific utterances by clinical consultants in each field. Current gap analysis shows the strongest performance in emergency and cardiac contexts (WER 1.4–1.8%), with slightly higher error rates in oncology and haematology (2.8%) due to the complexity of chemotherapy regimen nomenclature — an area of active improvement in the upcoming vocabulary release.

Radiology, Pathology, and Psychiatry are on the product roadmap but have not been formally validated in the current release. Customers deploying in these specialties during the pilot period are offered a co-development agreement, contributing de-identified interaction data in exchange for priority access to specialty vocabulary packs when released.

How does the Predictive Mortality Engine generate its risk scores?

The current implementation uses a gradient-boosted ensemble model trained on 340,000 ICU admission records from three academic medical centres, using the MIMIC-IV public dataset as the primary training corpus supplemented by de-identified pilot data. The model ingests a rolling 4-hour window of heart rate, SpO2, respiratory rate, blood pressure, and temperature — updated at 1-minute intervals from the patient's bedside monitor via the HL7 ADT/ORM interface.

The risk score is output as a probability (0–100%) of cardiovascular collapse within the next 30 minutes, calibrated against the pilot data's base rate. Risk tiers are: LOW (<15%), MODERATE (15–45%), HIGH (45–75%), and CRITICAL (>75%). At the CRITICAL threshold, the system automatically escalates to Sparsha's alert pipeline with a CRITICAL priority designation.

The 30-minute predictive window represents the time at which the model's receiver operating characteristic curve achieves AUC 0.89 — the point of maximum clinical utility. At shorter windows (10 minutes), AUC improves to 0.94 but provides insufficient intervention time. Model retraining occurs quarterly using accumulated pilot data, with performance regression testing before each update is deployed to production.

What does a VAPI tool call schema look like for alert dispatch?

Tool calls are the mechanism by which the VAPI AI layer triggers real-world actions — they are JSON-structured function calls that the AI emits when its intent classification determines that an action is required, rather than a conversational response.

// Tool definition registered with VAPI assistant
{
  "name": "send_alert",
  "description": "Dispatch emergency alert to ward staff",
  "parameters": {
    "staff_name": { "type": "string" },
    "room_number": { "type": "string" },
    "reason":      { "type": "string" },
    "priority":    { "type": "string" }
  }
}

When the AI emits this tool call, the useVapiVoice hook intercepts the tool-calls message, extracts the arguments, and POSTs them to /api/alerts on the local Express backend, which broadcasts the alert via SSE to all connected receiver dashboards. The deduplication layer in the hook (keyed on the VAPI-assigned tool call ID) prevents duplicate alerts from network retransmissions.

How is the wake word configured and what false-activation rate should we expect?

The default wake phrase is "Sparsha" — a deliberately short, phonetically distinct word that performs well against common ward noise profiles including HVAC systems, monitor alarm frequencies, and conversational background speech at 40–60dB SPL. The wake word model is a lightweight binary classifier running entirely on-device with a 12MB footprint, adding negligible load to the ward workstation.

In our validation dataset (800 hours of annotated ward audio across four hospital environments), the default wake word achieved: False Activation Rate of 0.003 per hour (roughly one spurious activation per two full shifts), True Activation Rate of 99.7% (99.7% of genuine "Sparsha" utterances at any speaking volume above 45dB at 1m are detected), and False Rejection Rate of 0.3%.

Custom wake phrases are available in Professional and Enterprise plans. The custom model training requires 200+ positive examples recorded by ward staff (a 20-minute recording session), plus 400 hours of negative examples drawn from your ward's ambient audio profile. Custom model training takes 3 business days and the resulting model is specific to your deployment. Departments with strong accents or bilingual staff often see 15–20% improvement in wake word accuracy with custom models versus the default.

Can the LLM be swapped for a different model without redeployment?

Yes. The LLM layer is abstracted behind a provider interface that supports hot-swapping without service restart. In the VAPI path, the underlying model is configured in the VAPI assistant settings — switching from GPT-4o-mini to GPT-4o, Claude Sonnet, or Gemini Pro is a configuration change that takes effect on the next session initiation. No code deployment is required.

In the Ollama local inference path, models are managed via Ollama's model registry. Switching from Gemma2:2b to Llama3:8b or Phi-3:medium requires pulling the new model (ollama pull llama3:8b) and updating the model identifier in the server configuration. The service can hot-reload the configuration without restarting, so the switch is transparent to active sessions, which complete against the previous model.

Enterprise customers with on-premise deployments can integrate privately hosted models via an OpenAI-compatible API shim. Any model serving an OpenAI-format completion API (including locally deployed fine-tuned models via vLLM or TGI) can be registered as a provider without modifying the Sparsha application code. Model-specific prompt templates for known clinical models (BioMedLM, MedPaLM, ClinicalBERT) are maintained in our template library.

What is the implementation timeline from contract to go-live?

The standard Professional plan implementation follows a structured 6-week programme. Week 1 covers infrastructure setup, network assessment, and VAPI/Groq API credential provisioning. Week 2 covers EMR integration scoping and HL7/FHIR connection establishment with the hospital's IT team. Week 3 covers ward-specific configuration: wake word calibration, intent template customization, alert routing rules, and RBAC definition. Week 4 covers staff training and the parallel pilot — Sparsha runs alongside the existing workflow, with side-by-side comparison of alert times and accuracy. Week 5 is a supervised go-live with the Sparsha integration specialist on-site for the first 40 hours of live operation. Week 6 covers performance review, tuning adjustments, and handover to ongoing support.

The 6-week programme assumes adequate IT resource from the hospital (approximately 20 hours of IT staff time total) and a single point of clinical contact who can provide ward workflow context and sign off on alert routing configurations. Hospitals with more complex infrastructure (on-premise EMR, private network segments, stringent change management processes) should budget 8–10 weeks.

What is the multimodal roadmap beyond voice?

The voice channel is the foundation because it is the natural language of clinical emergencies — it requires no device pickup, no interface navigation, and no hands freed from a patient. But clinical intelligence extends beyond audio. Our 18-month roadmap includes four multimodal expansions.

First: Vision-augmented triage. Camera feeds from ward corridors and patient bays will be processed by lightweight pose estimation and gait analysis models to detect falls, behavioural changes, and mobility impairments without facial recognition or persistent recording. Second: Wearable integration. Direct Bluetooth Low Energy ingestion from patient wearables (continuous glucose monitors, Holter devices, pulse oximeters) will feed the Predictive Mortality Engine with higher-frequency data than bedside monitors currently provide.

Third: Radiograph annotation assistance. Chest X-ray and CT preliminary read assistance — not autonomous diagnosis, but structured annotation of significant findings for radiologist review — using fine-tuned vision models trained on labelled radiology datasets. Fourth: Handwriting OCR for medication reconciliation. Capturing legacy paper prescription and medication chart data via camera and converting to structured medication records for EMR injection — closing the last analogue gap in the documentation workflow.
